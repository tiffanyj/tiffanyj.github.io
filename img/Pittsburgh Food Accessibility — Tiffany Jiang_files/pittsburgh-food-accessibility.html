Combating Food Accessibility in Pittsburgh 

User Research / Case Study

Many low-income Pittsburgh residents have difficulties getting access to fresh and health food options today. Our team hopes to develop a sustainable solution that teaches clients to be independent in how they obtain their food, reducing dependency on food banks and kitchens in the long-run. 

This eight week project was completed in Spring 2017 as a part of the curriculum for the HCI class, User Centered Research and Evaluation. The team consisted of two other user researchers: Maggie Banks (Product Design and HCI) and Grace Guo (Cognitive Science and HCI).

After assessing our findings concerning food accessibility, education and sustainability, our team came up with various interventions for ways to improve the experience of visiting a food bank or kitchen for all stakeholders involved.

For our final outcomes, we developed storyboards to communicate our ideas to stakeholders. We proposed a new way for members to exchange information on nutrition and food within the community. We also created a framework for a Co-op program that could be adopted by food banks to reduce visitor dependency on resources in the long-run.

A few months ago, I couldn't tell you what constitutes a food desert in an urban area. I had no idea that so much of Pittsburgh itself is made up of food deserts, despite all of the restaurants and eateries around town.

First, we researched what we could on the state of food deserts on both a nation-wide and city-wide scale. After equipping ourselves with enough basic information, we went out into the field to interview actual members of the community. We reached out to organizers and members of local food banks in the area to better understand the situation from their perspective over the course of several weeks.

Food Banks = Band-Aid Solutions

A key finding we discovered was that currently, the solutions in place act only as a temporary band-aid and don't tackle the issues holistically on a national scale. Food banks and community gardens, for example, really only benefit those who are nearby and have easy access to them.

If we want to help those in need, the solutions have to be sustainable in providing longterm access to healthy, fresh produce. These individuals should not have to put in extra work just because they lack transportation and are situationally disadvantaged.

Main Issue: Lack of Transportation 

From our literature review, we gathered further insights on the lives of those living in food deserts and the challenges that they face in getting groceries. Pittsburgh is notorious for its hilly terrain and harsh weather conditions. This makes going to the grocery store, which usually is over a mile away, more difficult both physically and psychologically.

When we started interviewing members of the local food kitchens and pantries, we found that many of them have a strong desire to eat well. But the lack of access to transportation gets in the way of doing so.

These issues are important to care about because the effects impact more than just those who are struggling to obtain a steady source of food. Providing food to the hungry is only one solution out of many. but does not get rid of the problem entirely. An entire community needs to get involved in order to elicit some measure of change.
